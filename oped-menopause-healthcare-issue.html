Menopause Is a Healthcare Issue.
It's Time We Started Treating It Like One.

B
Brooke Lunney Director of Communications, RevelleCare Inc.

Published July 2026  ·  4 min read

For generations, women have been told that menopause is simply something to endure. That explanation overlooks an important truth — and it's time we changed the conversation.

Hot flashes? Normal. Sleep disruption? Normal. Brain fog? Normal. Mood changes? Normal. Skin changes? That's just aging.

The message has been remarkably consistent: this is simply what happens as women get older. But that explanation overlooks an important truth.

Menopause is not simply an inevitable stage of aging. It is a significant biological transition driven by changing hormone levels, with effects that can influence nearly every system in the body. Yet despite affecting millions of Canadian women, menopause has historically received far less attention than many other stages of health.

For too long, conversations about menopause have been minimized, dismissed or treated as uncomfortable topics rather than legitimate healthcare concerns. Many women spend years trying to understand what is happening to their bodies before receiving clear answers. Others are told their symptoms are simply part of getting older and that little can be done.

That experience should concern all of us.

"When women are left without information, they are also left without choices. Better education is one of the most powerful ways we can improve menopause care in Canada."

Women deserve to understand what changes may occur during perimenopause and menopause before they experience them. They deserve evidence-informed resources that help distinguish common symptoms from issues that warrant medical attention. They deserve healthcare professionals who are equipped to have informed conversations and guide patients toward appropriate treatment options when needed.

Menopause should not be viewed as a niche issue affecting only a small segment of the population. Every woman who lives long enough will experience it. Families experience it. Employers are affected by it. Healthcare systems respond to it.

Yet public understanding often remains limited. This is beginning to change.

Across Canada and around the world, researchers, clinicians, educators and advocates are bringing greater attention to menopause and its impact on women's health. New research continues to improve our understanding of hormonal changes and their effects. Digital health innovations are creating new opportunities to connect patients with specialized care. Employers are beginning to recognize that supporting women through midlife is not only the right thing to do — it is good for workplaces, too.

These are encouraging developments. But awareness alone is not enough.

Improving menopause care requires a broader cultural shift. It means recognizing menopause as an important stage of health rather than an afterthought. It means investing in education, expanding access to evidence-informed care, encouraging research, and creating environments where women feel comfortable asking questions and seeking support. Most importantly, it means listening to women.

Their experiences should not be dismissed as inevitable consequences of aging. They should be understood, respected and addressed with the same seriousness we bring to other aspects of healthcare.

Every woman deserves to move through this stage of life with accurate information, compassionate care and confidence that her concerns will be taken seriously.

Canada has made remarkable progress in advancing women's health over the past several decades. Now it's time to make menopause part of that progress — because menopause is not simply a milestone in aging. It is a healthcare issue, and it's time we started treating it like one.
