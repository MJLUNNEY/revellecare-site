What Canada Can Do to
Improve Menopause Care

B
Brooke Lunney Director of Communications, RevelleCare Inc.

Published July 2026  ·  5 min read

Improving menopause care in Canada doesn't require reinventing our healthcare system. It requires recognizing menopause for what it is — and five concrete places where meaningful progress can begin.

Canada has made meaningful progress in women's health over the past several decades. We've invested in maternal health, improved breast cancer screening, expanded conversations around mental health and increased research into conditions that disproportionately affect women.

But one stage of a woman's life has remained surprisingly overlooked: menopause.

Every woman who lives long enough will experience menopause, yet too many still find themselves navigating it with limited information, inconsistent care and the feeling that they're expected to simply endure it. That can, and should, change.

Improving menopause care in Canada doesn't require reinventing our healthcare system. It requires recognizing menopause for what it is: a significant stage of health that deserves the same thoughtful attention we give to other life transitions.

Here are five places where meaningful progress can begin.

1

Normalize the conversation

For generations, menopause has often been discussed quietly, if at all. Many women reach perimenopause without fully understanding the changes they may experience because the topic simply wasn't part of everyday conversations. Education should begin long before symptoms appear. Just as we educate people about puberty, pregnancy and healthy aging, menopause deserves to be part of routine health education. Better awareness helps women recognize changes earlier, ask informed questions and seek appropriate care when they need it.

2

Strengthen education for healthcare professionals

Healthcare providers work incredibly hard to support their patients, but medicine is constantly evolving. As our understanding of menopause grows, continuing education should grow with it. Ensuring that physicians, nurse practitioners, pharmacists and other healthcare professionals have access to current, evidence-informed education benefits everyone. Women deserve confidence that their concerns will be heard, understood and addressed with the latest available knowledge.

3

Improve access to care

Canada is a large country and access to specialized healthcare can vary depending on where someone lives. Digital health has opened new possibilities for connecting patients with healthcare professionals, particularly for women in rural, remote or underserved communities. While virtual care will never replace every aspect of in-person medicine, it has become an important part of modern healthcare and can help improve access when integrated thoughtfully into the system. Innovation should complement our healthcare system, not compete with it.

4

Continue investing in research

For far too long, many aspects of women's health have received less research attention than they deserve. Every new study improves our understanding of how menopause affects physical health, mental well-being, skin, sleep, cardiovascular health, bone health and quality of life. Continued investment in research benefits not only today's patients, but future generations of women as well. Evidence should continue guiding conversations, policies and clinical care.

5

Listen to women

Perhaps the most important step of all is also the simplest. Believe women when they tell us what they're experiencing. Every menopause journey is different. Some women experience relatively few symptoms. Others face challenges that significantly affect their daily lives, careers, relationships and overall well-being. Those experiences deserve thoughtful conversations, not dismissal. When women feel heard, they are more likely to seek care, ask questions and make informed decisions about their health.

"Women spend nearly one-third of their lives in the post-menopausal stage. That alone should remind us that menopause is not a niche issue. It is a public health issue."

Canada has an opportunity to become a leader in menopause care. We have world-class researchers, dedicated healthcare professionals, innovative entrepreneurs and advocacy organizations working toward the same goal: helping women live healthier lives.

Progress won't come from one organization or one solution alone. It will come from collaboration. From education. From research. From innovation. And from a shared belief that menopause deserves a more prominent place in our national conversation about health.

Women spend nearly one-third of their lives in the post-menopausal stage. That alone should remind us that menopause is not a niche issue. It is a public health issue.

If we want healthier communities, healthier workplaces and healthier futures for Canadian women, improving menopause care isn't simply an opportunity. It's a responsibility. The conversation has already begun. Now it's time to turn that conversation into action.
